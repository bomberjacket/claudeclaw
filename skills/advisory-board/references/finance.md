# Finance Advisor

## Role

CFO-level thinking for solo founders who can't afford a CFO. Turns gut-feel pricing into data-driven decisions, makes runway math visceral, and ensures the business model actually works on paper before you scale it.

---

## Core Frameworks

### The Monetization Priority (inspired by Patrick Campbell)

**What it is:** Monetization improvements have 4x the impact of acquisition and 2x the impact of retention on revenue growth. Most founders obsess over getting more customers when their pricing is broken.

**How to apply:**
1. Before spending on growth, audit your pricing — when did you last change it?
2. Run willingness-to-pay surveys: "At what price would this be too expensive? Too cheap? A great deal? Getting expensive but still worth it?"
3. Price based on value delivered, not cost incurred or competitor matching
4. Revisit pricing every 6 months minimum

**Watch out for:** Using "we'll figure out pricing later" as an excuse to avoid the uncomfortable conversation of asking for money.

---

### Unit Economics (inspired by Patrick Campbell / Ben Murray)

**What it is:** The math of whether each customer is profitable. If your unit economics don't work at 100 customers, they won't magically work at 10,000.

**Key metrics:**
- **LTV (Lifetime Value):** Average revenue per customer × average lifespan
- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost):** Total sales + marketing spend ÷ new customers acquired
- **LTV:CAC Ratio:** Target 3:1+ for healthy business. Below 1:1 = paying to lose money
- **Payback Period:** Months to recover CAC. Under 12 months for bootstrapped founders
- **Gross Margin:** Revenue minus direct costs of delivery. Target 70%+ for SaaS, 50%+ for services

**How to apply:**
1. Calculate these for your business today — even rough estimates
2. If LTV:CAC < 3:1, fix pricing or reduce acquisition cost before scaling
3. If payback > 12 months, you need funding or a pricing change
4. Track monthly and watch trends, not just snapshots

**Watch out for:** Blended averages that hide segment differences. Your best customers might be 10:1 LTV:CAC while your worst are 0.5:1. Segment and serve the best.

### SaaS Metrics Dashboard (inspired by Ben Murray / "The SaaS CFO")

**What it is:** The key financial metrics every SaaS founder must track monthly.

**The essential dashboard:**
- **MRR / ARR:** Monthly/Annual Recurring Revenue — your baseline
- **MRR Growth Rate:** Month-over-month. 10-15% MoM is strong for early-stage
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR):** Revenue from existing customers this period vs last. 100%+ means customers are expanding. 120%+ is elite
- **Gross Churn:** Customers lost ÷ total customers. Under 5% monthly for SMB, under 2% for enterprise
- **Burn Rate:** Monthly cash spend. Know this number in your sleep
- **Runway:** Cash in bank ÷ monthly burn. Under 6 months = urgent action needed

**How to apply:**
1. Set up a simple spreadsheet tracking these monthly
2. Compare to benchmarks for your stage and market
3. The trend matters more than the absolute number
4. Focus on 1-2 metrics at a time based on your biggest constraint

### Five Pillar SaaS Metrics (inspired by Ben Murray / "The SaaS CFO")

**What it is:** Five pillars that give you a complete financial picture of a recurring revenue business.

1. **Growth:** MRR/ARR, new customer acquisition rate, expansion revenue
2. **Retention:** Revenue churn, logo churn, NRR, cohort retention curves
3. **Sales Efficiency:** CAC, CAC payback, Magic Number (net new ARR ÷ S&M spend; >0.75 is good)
4. **Profitability:** Gross margin, contribution margin, Rule of 40 (growth rate + profit margin ≥ 40%)
5. **Cash:** Burn rate, runway, cash conversion cycle, Burn Multiple (net burn ÷ net new ARR; <1.5x is efficient)

**How to apply:**
1. Track 1-2 metrics from each pillar monthly
2. Diagnose problems by pillar: "Is this a growth problem, a retention problem, or an efficiency problem?"
3. Fix one pillar at a time — usually retention first, then efficiency, then growth

**Watch out for:** Growth masking poor retention. If you're adding 10 customers and losing 8, your growth metrics look OK but your business is dying.

### SaaS Benchmarking: Five Ways to Build (inspired by Christoph Janz)

**What it is:** The animal framework — your average contract value determines your entire go-to-market strategy.

| Animal | ACV | Customers for $100M ARR | Sales Motion |
|--------|-----|------------------------|-------------|
| **Elephants** | $500K+ | 200 | Enterprise sales team |
| **Moose** | $50K | 2,000 | Inside sales + AEs |
| **Rabbits** | $5K | 20,000 | Self-serve + sales assist |
| **Mice** | $500 | 200,000 | Pure self-serve |
| **Flies** | $50 | 2,000,000 | Viral / consumer |

**How to apply:**
1. Know your animal — it determines everything (sales motion, marketing, support model)
2. Benchmark against companies with the same animal, not all SaaS
3. Most solo founders should target Rabbits or Mice — Moose and Elephants need sales teams

### Profit First Method (inspired by Mike Michalowicz / Cyndi Thomason)

**What it is:** Allocate revenue to profit, owner pay, taxes, and operating expenses in that order — before spending on anything else. The opposite of "revenue minus expenses = hopefully profit."

**How to apply:**
1. Set up separate bank accounts: Revenue, Profit (5-15%), Owner Pay, Tax (15-25%), Operating Expenses
2. On the 10th and 25th of each month, allocate incoming revenue by percentages
3. Operating expenses get what's LEFT, not what's "needed" — this forces discipline
4. Increase profit allocation by 1% each quarter

**Watch out for:** Treating this as optional when revenue is low. That's exactly when the habit matters most — it builds the discipline before the money arrives.

### Pricing Architecture (inspired by Patrick Campbell)

**What it is:** How you structure pricing to capture maximum value from different customer segments.

**Key principles:**
- **Value metric:** Charge based on the unit of value you deliver (seats, usage, transactions) — not flat rate
- **Good-Better-Best:** Three tiers work better than one or two. The middle tier is your target; the top tier is your anchor
- **Price anchoring:** Show the expensive option first. The middle option becomes "reasonable"
- **Localization:** Different markets, different willingness to pay. Don't leave money on the table

**How to apply:**
1. Identify your value metric: what unit does the customer care about?
2. Design 3 tiers: Starter (gets them in), Pro (where most land), Enterprise (high-touch, high-value)
3. Price the Pro tier at your target willingness-to-pay, Starter at 50-60% of Pro, Enterprise at 2-3x Pro
4. Test with real prospects: "Would you pay $X for this?" Watch behavior, not just words

### Simple Numbers: Profitability-First P&L (inspired by Greg Crabtree)

**What it is:** Traditional accounting is designed for tax minimization, not for revealing economic truth. Greg Crabtree's "Simple Numbers" framework restructures your P&L to expose whether the business is actually a wealth-building machine or just an expensive job.

**The Bus Test:** "If you got hit by a bus and your family had to hire someone to do your job, what would that person cost?" That's your market-based salary. Pay yourself that first — before calculating profit. Most founders skip this and call the leftover "profit," which is a lie.

**Key rule:** 10% pretax profit (after paying yourself a market wage) is the new breakeven. Below that, the business can't fund taxes, debt reduction, or reserves. You're slowly dying.

| Pretax Profit | Health Status | What It Means |
|---------------|--------------|---------------|
| ≤ 5% | Life Support | Cut costs or raise prices immediately |
| 10% | Healthy | Sustainable but limited growth capital |
| 15% | Great | Authorized to invest in growth and hiring |
| 20-25% | Target | Optimal wealth creation — industry leader territory |

**How to apply:**
1. Set your market-based owner salary and put yourself on payroll at that number
2. Separate labor costs from all other COGS — labor is your primary lever, don't bury it
3. Calculate pretax profit AFTER your market salary. That's the real number.
4. If it's below 10%, fix profitability before growth

### Labor Efficiency Ratio (inspired by Greg Crabtree)

**What it is:** For every dollar you spend on labor, how many dollars of gross margin does it produce? This is the single most important operational metric for service and small SaaS businesses.

| Metric | Formula | Target |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Total LER** | Gross Margin ÷ Total Labor Cost | 2.0 minimum (below = "life support") |
| **Direct LER** | Gross Margin ÷ Direct Labor Cost | 3.0+ (frontline productivity) |
| **Management LER** | Contribution Margin ÷ Management Labor Cost | 4.0+ (back-office efficiency) |

**Industry benchmarks:**
- Service & Consulting: 2.0 – 2.5 (high labor dependency)
- SaaS & Tech: 2.5+ (scalability allows superior labor leverage)
- Retail & Distribution: 2.5+ (thin margins need efficient labor)

**How to apply:**
1. Calculate your LER today — even a rough estimate reveals whether labor is productive
2. If Total LER is below 2.0, you're either underpriced or overstaffed
3. Track monthly — declining LER signals pricing erosion or a hire that isn't producing
4. Use LER to evaluate every hire: "Will this person improve or worsen our ratio?"

**Watch out for:** Mixing labor into COGS where it gets hidden. Isolate labor as its own line item — that's where the insight lives.

### The Four Forces of Cash Flow (inspired by Greg Crabtree)

**What it is:** A priority order for how profit should flow through the business. Most founders distribute first and save last — Crabtree inverts it.

| Priority | Force | Rule |
|----------|-------|------|
| 1st | Pay Taxes | Set aside as profit is earned — "Uncle Sam is not a good lender" |
| 2nd | Retire Debt | Eliminate lines of credit and high-interest debt |
| 3rd | Build Core Capital | Two months of operating expenses in cash before any distributions |
| 4th | Distribute Profit | Only after the first three are fully satisfied |

**Core Capital rule:** If you don't have 2 months of operating expenses in cash with zero draw on a line of credit, you're undercapitalized. Every decision should account for this.

### The Scaling Decision Framework (inspired by Greg Crabtree)

**What it is:** A profitability-gated hiring model. Don't hire based on revenue — hire based on margin.

1. Reach 15% pretax profit before authorizing any new hire
2. Calculate the **Salary Cap** — total labor budget derived from your target profit margin, not revenue
3. Hire for function, not familiarity — fill the specific gap (Ops, Sales, CS), don't clone yourself
4. Expect the dip — profit will drop toward 10% after a new hire; drive efficiency back to 15% before hiring again
5. Apply the **50% ROIC filter** — if the hire doesn't have a clear path to 50% return on invested capital, reject it

**The Black Hole ($1M-$5M):** Revenue growing while profit margin shrinks. This is where most small businesses get stuck — they add people and overhead faster than they add profitable revenue. LER is the diagnostic.

### Fractional CFO Framework (inspired by Chelsea Williams)

**What it is:** The financial disciplines a solo founder needs even without a finance team.

**Monthly financial rhythm:**
1. **Week 1:** Review last month's P&L. What surprised you?
2. **Week 2:** Cash flow forecast — 13-week rolling. When does cash get tight?
3. **Week 3:** Review metrics dashboard (MRR, churn, LTV:CAC)
4. **Week 4:** Financial planning — what decisions need to be made?

**Key questions for every spending decision:**
- What's the expected return and over what timeframe?
- Is this reversible if it doesn't work?
- Does this improve unit economics or worsen them?
- Can I fund this from operating cash flow or does it require reserves?

### Runway Decision Framework

**What it is:** A decision tree based on how much runway you have.

| Runway | Priority | Anti-Pattern |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **12+ months** | Invest in growth, experiment freely | Hoarding cash while competitors move |
| **6-12 months** | Focus spending, cut experiments that aren't working | Starting new initiatives without killing old ones |
| **3-6 months** | Survival mode — revenue generation only | Continuing to build features instead of selling |
| **< 3 months** | Emergency — cut costs, generate revenue immediately, or raise | Pretending things are fine |

**The Default Alive/Dead question (Paul Graham):** If you keep growing at your current rate with your current expenses, do you reach profitability before running out of money? If not, you're "default dead" — every decision should be evaluated through that lens.

### Unit Economics Health Check

| Metric | Healthy | Warning | Crisis |
|--------|---------|---------|--------|
| LTV:CAC | >3:1 | 2:1 - 3:1 | <2:1 |
| CAC Payback | <12 months | 12-18 months | >18 months |
| Monthly Churn | <5% | 5-8% | >8% |
| Gross Margin | >70% | 50-70% | <50% |
| Months Runway | >18 | 12-18 | <12 |
| Quick Ratio | >4 | 2-4 | <2 |

**Quick Ratio** = (New MRR + Expansion MRR) ÷ (Churned MRR + Contraction MRR). Measures the health of your growth — are you filling the bucket faster than it leaks?

## Diagnostic Questions

Before giving financial advice, understand:

1. What's your current MRR/revenue? (Even $0 is useful to know)
2. What's your monthly burn rate?
3. How much runway do you have?
4. How are you currently pricing? (Free, freemium, paid, usage-based?)
5. What's your gross margin on each sale?
6. Do you know your LTV and CAC? (Even rough estimates)
7. Are you bootstrapped or funded?
8. What's your revenue goal for the next 6 months?

## Red Flags This Advisor Calls Out

- "I'll figure out pricing later" — pricing IS the product for a bootstrapped founder
- Revenue growing but cash shrinking — check unit economics immediately
- Spending on growth with LTV:CAC below 3:1
- No runway visibility — flying blind toward a cliff
- Freemium without a clear conversion path to paid
- Undercharging because "the competitor charges less" without understanding value differential
- Hiring before unit economics support it
- Confusing revenue with profit
- Owner taking minimal salary but claiming the business is "profitable" — run the Bus Test
- Revenue growing while profit margin shrinks — the Black Hole (Crabtree)
- Making hiring decisions on gut feel instead of the Salary Cap and LER
- Using a line of credit to cover operating expenses — undercapitalized
- No market-based owner compensation — the P&L is lying to you

---

## When NOT to Use This Advisor

- Pure product/UX questions (that's Product)
- Brand and messaging decisions (that's Marketing)
- Code architecture decisions (that's Technical)
- Emotional/motivational questions (that's Founder Coach)
- When the founder has zero revenue AND zero users — start with Product and Sales first
